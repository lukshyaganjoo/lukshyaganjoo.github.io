## gram matrices are literally magic

### pairwise non-negative inner products

> Given $n + 2$ vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{n + 2} \in \R^n$,
> prove that there always $2$ distinct vectors $\mathbf{v}_i$ and $\mathbf{v}_j$ such
> that $\langle \mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{v}_j\rangle \geq 0$.

**Proof:** This one is gorgeous. Define the matrix

$$
\begin{align*}
    \mathbf{V} := \begin{bmatrix}
        \mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_{n + 2}
    \end{bmatrix} \in \R^{n \times (n + 2)}
\end{align*}
$$ and consider the corresponding Gram matrix
$\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{V} \in \R^{(n + 2) \times (n + 2)}$. Notice that $$\rank(\mathbf{G}) = \rank(\mathbf{V}^\top \mathbf{V}) \leq \text{rank}(\mathbf{V}) \leq n\,.$$ We argue via contradiction. Assume there are no distinct $i, j$ such that $\innerp{\mathbf{v}_i}{\mathbf{v}_j} \geq 0$. Therefore all the off-diagonal entries of $\mathbf{G}$ are strictly negative. Then, we have the matrix $\mathbf{M} = \left\{\max \limits_{1 \leq i \leq n + 2} (\|\mathbf{v}_i\|) + 1\right\} \Id - \mathbf{G}$ has strictly positive entries. By the Perron Frobenius theorem, it has unique maximal eigenvalue $\lambda_{\text{max}}$. Equivalently, $\mathbf{G}$ has a unique minimal eigenvalue $\lambda_{\text{min}}$. To see this put $c := \max \limits_{1 \leq i \leq n + 2} (\norm{\mathbf{v}_i}) + 1$ and
notice that $c \mathbb{Id}$ and $\mathbf{G}$ commute and therefore possess a shared eigenbasis. Denote this eigenbasis by $\{\mathbf{y}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_{n + 2}\}$. Since $\mathbf{M}$ is defined to be the sum of these matrices, it too possesses this eigenbasis. Let $\{\beta_i\}$ be the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{M}$ and $\{\lambda_i\}$ be the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{G}$. Noting that the eigenvalues of the identity are
all $1$, we have
$$\begin{align*}
    \beta_k \mathbf{y}_k = \mathbf{M} \mathbf{y}_k &= c \mathbf{y}_k - \lambda_k \mathbf{y}_k \implies \lambda_{\text{min}} = c - \beta_{\text{max}}
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\mathbf{G} \succeq 0$, its eigenvalues are $\geq 0$. However by the rank-constraint,
$\mathbf{G}$ has rank at most $n$ and therefore nullity at least $2$, implying that its minimal eigenvalue is non-unique. Contradiction!
