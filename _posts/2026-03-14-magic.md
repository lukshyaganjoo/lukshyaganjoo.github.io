## gram matrices are literally magic

### pairwise non-negative inner products

> Given $n + 2$ vectors $\vv_1, \vv_2, \ldots, \vv_{n + 2} \in \R^n$,
> prove that there always $2$ distinct vectors $\vv_i$ and $\vv_j$ such
> $\innerp{\vv_i}{\vv_j} \geq 0$.

**Proof:** This one is gorgeous. Define the matrix
$$
    \mathbf{V} = \begin{bmatrix}
        \vv_1 & \vv_2 & \cdots & \vv_{n + 2}
    \end{bmatrix} \in \R^{n \times (n + 2)}
    $$ and consider the corresponding Gram matrix 
    $\mathbf{G} = \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{V} \in \R^{(n + 2) \times (n + 2)}$. 
    Notice that 
    $$\rank(\mathbf{G}) = \rank(\mathbf{V}^\top \mathbf{V}) \leq \rank(\mathbf{V}) \leq n\,.$$
We argue via contradiction. Assume there are no distinct $i, j$ such that $\innerp{\vv_i}{\vv_j} \geq 0$. Therefore all the off-diagonal entries of $\mathbf{G}$ are strictly negative. Then, we have the matrix $\mathbf{M} = \left\{\max \limits_{1 \leq i \leq n + 2} (\norm{\vv_i}) + 1\right\} \Id - \mathbf{G}$ has strictly positive entries. By the Perron Frobenius theorem, it has unique maximal eigenvalue $\lambda_{\text{max}}$. Equivalently, $\mathbf{G}$ has a unique minimal eigenvalue $\lambda_{\text{min}}$. To see this put $c = \max \limits_{1 \leq i \leq n + 2} (\norm{\vv_i}) + 1$ and
notice that $c \Id$ and $\mathbf{G}$ commute and therefore possess a shared eigenbasis. Denote this eigenbasis by $\{\yy_1, \ldots, \yy_{n + 2}\}$. Since $\mathbf{M}$ is defined to be the sum of these matrices, it too possesses this eigenbasis. Let $\{\beta_i\}$ be the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{M}$ and $\{\lambda_i\}$ be the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{G}$. Noting that the eigenvalues of the identity are
all $1$, we have
$$\begin{align*}
        \beta_k \yy_k = \mathbf{M} \yy_k &= c \yy_k - \lambda_k \yy_k \implies \lambda_{\text{min}} = c - \beta_{\text{max}} 
    \end{align*}$$
Since $\mathbf{G} \succeq 0$, its eigenvalues are $\geq 0$. However by the rank-constraint,
$\mathbf{G}$ has rank at most $n$ and therefore nullity at least $2$, implying that its minimal eigenvalue is non-unique. Contradiction!
\end{proof}
